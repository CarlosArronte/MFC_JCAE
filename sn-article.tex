\abstract{Automotive safety has been a critical focus for both national and international research institutions in recent years. The combined control of lateral dynamics and roll stability in vehicles has been extensively studied, often relying on automotive models to design and tune controllers. However, these models frequently include parametric uncertainties, nonlinear dynamics, and simplifying assumptions, which can lead to suboptimal controller performance in real-world applications.
This work proposes a unified chassis controller (UCC) to regulate lateral and roll dynamics during abrupt maneuvers and evaluates a model-free control (MFC) strategy against representative benchmark controllers. The comparison includes a fixed-gain PID controller, a gain-scheduled linear quadratic regulator (LQR), and the built-in CarSim ESC baseline, all assessed within the same co-simulation environment combining MATLAB/Simulink\textsuperscript{\textregistered} and CarSim\textsuperscript{\textregistered}. Results demonstrate that while the benchmark controllers degrade under varying road conditions, the MFC-based UCC achieves superior robustness and performance, effectively regulating the state variables across different scenarios. These findings highlight the robustness and adaptability of MFCs, making them a promising solution for enhancing vehicle safety.
}

\section{Introduction}\label{intro}

Road traffic accidents remain a major global concern, causing approximately 1.3 million deaths 
and tens of millions of injuries each year \citep{UN2022}. Many of these incidents are associated 
with loss of vehicle stability during abrupt maneuvers, tire failures, or adverse road conditions 
\citep{Friedman2010}. Such situations are typically characterized by excessive side-slip angle and 
yaw rate, which can lead to oversteer or understeer, as well as increased rollover risk due to 
significant roll angle variations \citep{Stolle2011,zhan2017yawrate,nguyen2023rollover}. 
Consequently, side-slip angle, yaw rate, and roll angle have been widely recognized as the key 
state variables for vehicle stability monitoring and control, serving as the primary feedback 
signals in active safety systems \citep{Rajamani2012}. %Ok

To address these challenges, modern vehicles incorporate advanced control systems such as Electronic 
Stability Control (ESC), which improves lateral stability through selective braking and torque 
modulation \citep{Hac2007}. In parallel, suspension-based systems, including active and semi-active 
suspensions, are used to regulate vertical dynamics and enhance ride comfort.\

 Unified Chassis Control 
(UCC) extends these concepts by coordinating multiple subsystems to simultaneously regulate lateral 
and vertical vehicle dynamics, improving overall stability and safety, particularly under extreme 
driving conditions \citep{Cho2012}. However, achieving this coordination is non-trivial, as 
lateral stability enhancement, rollover mitigation, and actuator allocation are inherently competing 
objectives, requiring careful trade-off management to avoid control conflicts between subsystems 
\citep{Falcone2007}. %Ok

Most existing UCC strategies rely on model-based control approaches, which can be broadly 
categorized by their underlying vehicle representation. On one end, simplified formulations 
such as the bicycle model have been widely adopted within Model Predictive Control (MPC) 
frameworks, offering computational tractability for real-time path-following and yaw 
stabilization tasks \citep{Ahmadi2021,Gidlewski2017,Yakub2016}. On the other end, 
multi-degree-of-freedom formulations have been proposed to capture the coupling between 
lateral, vertical, and suspension dynamics more faithfully: \citet{Lai2021} developed an 
18-DOF chassis model that enables high-fidelity simulation of cross-subsystem interactions, 
while \citet{Chen2021} extended this perspective to electric vehicles by explicitly 
incorporating wheel vertical vibrations into a UCC architecture.\

Although these high-fidelity representations improve prediction accuracy and allow for 
a more principled treatment of lateral-vertical coupling, they introduce substantial 
computational overhead and exhibit strong sensitivity to parametric uncertainty, limiting 
their portability across varying road conditions and vehicle configurations 
\citep{Guo2020A,Lew2024Risk-Averse}. %Ok

To mitigate these limitations, several works have introduced uncertainty compensation mechanisms 
based on data-driven or adaptive techniques. Neural network-based controllers have been widely 
used to approximate unknown nonlinearities and disturbances in vehicle dynamics, enhancing 
robustness under varying conditions. Similarly, fuzzy and neuro-fuzzy approaches enable online 
adaptation of control parameters, while sliding mode control combined with intelligent 
approximators provides robustness against disturbances and model mismatch 
\citep{electronics10040510,Guo2021}.\ 

More recently, hybrid strategies combining model-based 
controllers with learning-based compensation terms have demonstrated improved performance in 
the presence of uncertainties \citep{levine2016end}. While these strategies collectively 
advance robustness against parametric uncertainty and external disturbances, a systematic 
and homogeneous comparison between them and model-free control approaches within a UCC architecture remains scarce in the literature, limiting the ability to draw 
generalizable conclusions about their relative merits under consistent evaluation conditions \citep{SanchezMorales2023,Mazzilli2021}.%ok


Despite the advances described above, the practical deployment of UCC strategies under varying operating conditions remains challenging. Model-based approaches, even when augmented with adaptive or learning-based compensation mechanisms, often remain sensitive to parametric uncertainty and typically require significant retuning when vehicle parameters or road conditions change.\ 

 In this context, model-free control (MFC) has emerged as a structurally simpler alternative that reduces dependence on an explicit vehicle model; however, its performance within a UCC architecture has only rarely been assessed against representative baselines. In particular, many comparative studies in this domain rely primarily on fixed-gain PID controllers, leaving open the question of how MFC compares with more meaningful references such as gain-scheduled controllers and, simulator-embedded baseline strategies alternatives under consistent evaluation conditions.\ 
 
 To address this gap, this paper proposes a model-free UCC strategy for the simultaneous regulation of side-slip angle, yaw rate, and roll angle, and evaluates it in a MATLAB/Simulink-CarSim co-simulation environment under a sine-with-dwell maneuver with different road friction coefficients. The main contributions of this work are threefold: first, the design and implementation of an MFC-based UCC architecture for the joint regulation of lateral and vertical vehicle dynamics without relying on an explicit vehicle model; second, a comparative assessment against representative benchmark controllers, including a fixed-gain PID, a gain-scheduled LQR, and a CarSim built-in baseline; and third, an analysis of practical closed-loop stability, robustness to friction variation, and control performance limits of the proposed approach. %OK

\section{Control Problem Formulation}\label{sec:problem}

This section formally defines the control problem addressed in this work. 
The regulated variables, reference generation strategy, actuator constraints, 
and maneuver evaluation criteria are described, establishing the framework 
within which the proposed UCC is designed and assessed.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Vehicle States and Control Objectives}\label{sec:states}

The vehicle dynamics relevant to this work are characterized by three state 
variables: the side-slip angle $\beta$, the yaw rate $\dot{\psi}$, and the 
roll angle $\phi$. Together, these variables capture the coupled lateral and 
vertical behavior of the chassis during abrupt maneuvers and serve as the 
primary feedback signals for the proposed UCC.

The side-slip angle is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    \beta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{V_y}{V_x}\right),
    \label{Eq:beta_def}
\end{equation}
where $V_y$ and $V_x$ are the lateral and longitudinal vehicle velocities, 
respectively. In the present co-simulation setup, both $V_x$ and $V_y$ are directly obtained from CarSim. Accordingly, $\beta$ is computed from ideal state information available in simulation, rather than being reconstructed from measured signals through an estimation algorithm. This choice is suitable for comparative controller assessment, although it abstracts from the state-estimation problem that would arise in a real-vehicle implementation.

The reference for $\beta$ is set to zero, reflecting the control objective 
of minimizing lateral drift. The controller acts only when the side-slip 
angle exceeds the safety threshold:
\begin{equation}
    |\beta| > \beta_{\mathrm{lim}} = 2^{\circ}.
    \label{Eq:beta_lim}
\end{equation}

The yaw rate reference $\dot{\psi}_D$ is computed dynamically based on 
longitudinal speed, front wheel steering angle, and road friction coefficient, 
as described in Section~\ref{sec:references}. The roll angle reference is 
always set to zero, as the control objective is to minimize chassis tilt 
throughout the maneuver.\ 

For the sine-with-dwell maneuver SWD considered in this study, choosing $\phi^* = 0$ is appropriate because the main control objective is to minimize body roll and preserve rollover safety margins during a severe transient maneuver, rather than to track a nonzero equilibrium roll angle. Under this perspective, a zero roll reference represents the nominal upright posture of the vehicle and allows the controller to directly counteract excessive lateral load transfer.


% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Reference Generation and Operating 
            Constraints}\label{sec:references}

The desired yaw rate is computed as:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{\psi}_D = \min\left(\left|\frac{\mu g}{V_x}\right|, 
    \left|G_{\dot{\psi}}\,\delta_f\right|\right) 
    \cdot \mathrm{sign}(\delta_f),
    \label{Eq:yaw_ref}
\end{equation}
where $G_{\dot{\psi}} = V_x / (L + K_{us} V_x^2)$ is the yaw rate gain, 
$L$ is the vehicle wheelbase, $K_{us}$ is the understeer coefficient, 
$\delta_f$ is the front wheel steering angle, $\mu$ is the road friction 
coefficient, and $g$ is the gravitational acceleration. The parameters required to compute $G_{\dot{\psi}}$ are taken from \cite{Arronte2023}. The same reference provides the full set of vehicle parameters used in the CarSim model considered in this work.\ 

In this study, the road friction coefficient $\mu$ is imposed as a simulation parameter and kept constant within each test scenario. Therefore, $\mu$ is not estimated online, but prescribed a priori in CarSim to define the operating condition under which the controllers are evaluated. This choice is consistent with the main objective of the experiments, namely, to assess controller performance and robustness under different known friction levels.


% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Control Inputs and Actuator 
            Constraints}\label{sec:actuators}

The high-level controller produces two virtual control inputs: a yaw moment 
$M_z$, acting about the vehicle's vertical axis to regulate lateral dynamics, 
and a roll moment $M_x$, acting about the longitudinal axis to regulate 
vertical dynamics. These moments are subject to the following saturation 
constraints:
\begin{equation}
    M_z \in [-8000,\; 8000] \;\text{Nm},
    \label{Eq:Mz_sat}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    M_x \in [-1500,\; 1500] \;\text{Nm}.
    \label{Eq:Mx_sat}
\end{equation}

The saturation limits were set in accordance with the maximum 
torque capacity of the ABS and AARB subsystems as defined in the 
CarSim\textsuperscript{\textregistered} SUV model used throughout 
this work, consistent with the simulation framework established 
in \citep{Arronte2023}.\

At the actuation level, $M_z$ is decomposed into individual wheel brake 
pressure commands by the ABS subsystem, while $M_x$ is translated into 
active anti-roll bar (AARB) force commands. Both decompositions are 
handled internally by CarSim\textsuperscript{\textregistered}, as described 
in Section~\ref{sec:ucc}.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Maneuver Completion and Failure 
            Criteria}\label{sec:criteria}

The performance of the UCC is evaluated under a Sine With Dwell (SWD) 
maneuver, a standardized test used to assess vehicle response to sudden 
evasive inputs, as specified in FMVSS 126 and UN/ECE Regulation No. 13-H 
\citep{MSC2012}. A maneuver is considered successfully completed if the 
vehicle maintains controlled motion throughout the full duration of the 
steering input without loss of stability.\

In the context of the SWD maneuver, maneuver failure is determined 
following the criteria established by FMVSS 126 and implemented in 
CarSim\textsuperscript{\textregistered} \citep{carsimintro2021}: 
the vehicle is considered to have failed the maneuver if the yaw rate 
measured at 1.0\,s after the completion of steering exceeds 35\% of 
the peak yaw rate recorded during the maneuver, or if the yaw rate 
at 1.75\,s after the completion of steering exceeds 20\% of that peak 
value \citep{MSC2012}. In practice, within the CarSim co-simulation 
environment, this condition manifests as an abrupt termination of the 
simulation due to vehicle loss of control, which is the failure 
indicator used throughout Section~\ref{sec2}.

In addition to maneuver completion, controller performance is quantified 
using the root mean square (RMS) of each regulated variable, the root mean 
square error (RMSE) with respect to the corresponding reference, and the 
maximum absolute value (MAX) recorded during the maneuver. Control effort 
is monitored through the time histories of $M_z$ and $M_x$, with particular 
attention to saturation occurrence and duration.%Ojo a las metricas de esf ctrl (hacerlas coincidir con experimentos)

\section{Benchmark Controllers and Comparative Framework}\label{sec:bench}

To provide a rigorous and meaningful assessment of the proposed MFC-based 
UCC, three benchmark controllers are considered. Each controller operates 
within the same UCC architecture described in Section~\ref{sec:ucc}, 
receives the same input signals, and is subject to the same actuator 
saturation constraints defined in Section~\ref{sec:actuators}. The 
benchmarks are selected to represent progressively more capable reference 
strategies, allowing the comparison to go beyond the fixed-gain baselines 
that are common in the literature \citep{SanchezMorales2023}.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Fixed-Gain PID Controller}\label{sec:pid}

The first benchmark is a fixed-gain PID controller previously designed and 
validated in \citep{Arronte2023}. 
In that design, the ESC benchmark regulates the side-slip angle $\beta$ 
and the yaw rate $\dot{\psi}$ through two PID branches with hysteresis 
that generate a corrective yaw moment implemented by differential wheel 
braking. By contrast, the roll angle $\phi$ is not directly regulated in 
\citep{Arronte2023}$:$ it is only monitored as part of the vehicle 
response. Accordingly, in the present comparison the fixed-gain PID 
benchmark has no dedicated roll-control channel, and the roll-related 
results must be interpreted as an indirect consequence of lateral 
stabilization rather than as the outcome of explicit roll regulation.\\
The controller parameters reported in \citep[Sec.~III-E, 
Eqs.~(19)--(20)]{Arronte2023} are as follows. For the side-slip 
channel, the gains are $K_P = 5.0 \times 10^4$, $K_I = 0.01$, and 
$K_D = 5.0 \times 10^3$. For the yaw-rate channel, the gains are 
$K_P = 1.0 \times 10^5$, $K_I = 0.01$, and $K_D = 2.0 \times 10^3$. 
In both cases, the derivative term is implemented with a filter of 
order $N = 100$.

The tuning procedure in \citep{Arronte2023} combines analytical 
linearization with empirical adjustment rather than relying on a 
standard heuristic such as Ziegler--Nichols. The plant is first 
linearized under the assumptions that the lateral forces are 
proportional to the cornering stiffness and that $\beta \approx v/u_0$. 
Based on this approximation, the proportional gains are selected from 
desired closed-loop poles of $-12.5$ for the side-slip loop and $-22$ 
for the yaw-rate loop. The integral gain is kept small because 
eliminating steady-state error is not the primary objective during 
abrupt maneuvers, whereas $K_D$ and the derivative filter parameter $N$ 
are adjusted by trial and error to reduce error oscillations and smooth 
the control effort.

(LQR), in which the state-feedback gain matrix is adapted as a function

\section{Model-Free Unified Chassis Control 
         Design}\label{sec:mfc_ucc}

This section presents the proposed MFC-based UCC architecture. The 
formulation begins with the ultra-local model and the intelligent PID 
control law, followed by the algebraic estimators for the lumped 
disturbance term, the overall UCC architecture, and the coordination 
and control allocation strategies. The section concludes with the 
parameter selection procedure.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Ultra-Local Model and Intelligent PID 
            Formulation}\label{sec:ulmodel}

The MFC framework adopted in this work is based on the ultra-local model 
introduced by \citet{Fliess2008}, which assumes that the plant input-output 
behavior can be approximated over a short time window as:
\begin{equation}
    y^{(v)} = F + \alpha u,
    \label{Eq:Def-MFC}
\end{equation}
where $y^{(v)}$ denotes the $v$-th time derivative of the output, $F$ is 
a lumped term that captures the unknown plant dynamics, external 
disturbances, and unmodeled nonlinearities, $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$ is a 
non-physical scaling parameter chosen by the designer, and $u$ is the 
control input \citep{Fliess2009}. The term $F$ is treated as a 
piecewise-constant function updated at each estimation window, making 
the representation independent of any explicit vehicle model.

In this work, three plant variables are regulated: the yaw rate 
$\dot{\psi}$, the side-slip angle $\beta$, and the roll angle $\phi$. 
The yaw rate channel is represented by a first-order ultra-local model 
($v = 1$), while the side-slip angle and roll angle channels use a 
second-order formulation ($v = 2$).

%TODO: agregar una justificación breve de por qué $\dot{\psi}$ se 
%      modela con $v=1$ y $\beta$, $\phi$ con $v=2$. Una oración 
%      es suficiente — por ejemplo, en términos de la relación 
%      entrada-salida observable o del grado relativo del sistema.

Assuming that the ultra-local model holds over a short time window, 
the stabilizing intelligent PID (iPID) control law is given by:
\begin{equation}
    u = \frac{-F + \ddot{y}^*}{\alpha} + K_P e + K_I \int e \, dt 
        + K_D \frac{de}{dt},
    \label{Def-u-v2}
\end{equation}
where $y^*$ is the reference trajectory, $e = y - y^*$ is the tracking 
error, and $K_P$, $K_I$, $K_D$ are the PID gains. In this work, the 
integral term is set to zero ($K_I = 0$), reducing the controller to an 
intelligent PD (iPD) structure. The term $-F/\alpha$ acts as an online 
feedforward compensation that cancels the estimated lumped dynamics, 
while the PD component handles residual tracking errors.

\subsection{Algebraic Estimation of the Lumped 
            Term}\label{sec:estimators}

The real-time estimation of $F$ is performed using an algebraic approach 
derived from the ultra-local model via Laplace-domain manipulations, 
following \citet{Fliess2009}. The estimators are expressed as weighted 
integrals of the input and output signals over a sliding window of 
length $T$, which provides inherent noise attenuation without requiring 
explicit filtering.

\subsubsection{Second-order estimator for $\beta$ and $\phi$}

For the second-order channels ($v = 2$), the lumped term is estimated as:
\begin{multline}
    F_2 = \frac{60}{t^5}\int_{0}^{t}(t^2 - 6t\tau + 6\tau^2)
          y(\tau)\,d\tau \\
        - \frac{30\alpha}{t^5}\int_{0}^{t}(t-\tau)^2\tau^2
          u(\tau)\,d\tau.
    \label{Antilaplace-Def-MFC-v2}
\end{multline}

\subsubsection{First-order estimator for $\dot{\psi}$}

For the first-order channel ($v = 1$), the estimator takes the form:
\begin{multline}
    F_1 = -\frac{6}{t^3}\int_{0}^{t}(t - 2\tau)y(\tau)\,d\tau \\
          - \frac{6\alpha}{t^3}\int_{0}^{t}(t-\tau)\tau\, 
          u(\tau)\,d\tau.
    \label{Antilaplace-Def-MFC-v1}
\end{multline}

\subsubsection{Discrete-time implementation}

For digital implementation, the integrals in 
Eqs.~(\ref{Antilaplace-Def-MFC-v2}) and (\ref{Antilaplace-Def-MFC-v1}) 
are approximated using the trapezoidal rule over a window of length $T$, 
sampled at period $T_s$. The discrete estimators are:
\begin{multline}
    \hat{F}_2 = \frac{60}{T^5}\sum_{k=0}^{T/T_s} a_k
                (T^2 - 6TkT_s + 6(kT_s)^2)y[k] \\
              - \frac{30\hat{\alpha}}{T^5}\sum_{k=0}^{T/T_s} 
                a_k(T - kT_s)^2(kT_s)^2 u[k],
\end{multline}
\begin{multline}
    \hat{F}_1 = -\frac{6}{T^3}\sum_{k=0}^{T/T_s} 
                a_k(T - 2kT_s)y[k] \\
              - \frac{6\hat{\alpha}}{T^3}\sum_{k=0}^{T/T_s}
                a_k(T - kT_s)kT_s\, u[k],
\end{multline}
where the trapezoidal weights are:
\begin{equation}
    a_k = \begin{cases}
        T_s/2, & k = 0 \text{ and } k = T/T_s, \\
        T_s,   & k = 1, \dots, T/T_s - 1.
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

The integration window was set to $T = 1.0$\,s for all three estimators, 
based on the characteristic response time of the vehicle dynamics under 
the SWD maneuver.

\subsection{Parameter Selection and Implementation 
            Details}\label{sec:params}

The iPD controllers involve three sets of parameters per channel: the 
scaling constant $\alpha$, the proportional gain $K_P$, and the derivative 
gain $K_D$. These were selected empirically through iterative simulation, 
guided by the following criteria: $\alpha$ was adjusted to match the 
order of magnitude of the unknown plant gain for each channel, while 
$K_P$ and $K_D$ were tuned to balance tracking performance and control 
signal smoothness, prioritizing maneuver safety over passenger comfort.

The effect of $\alpha$ on closed-loop behavior was observed to be 
monotonic: higher values introduce response delay, while lower values 
increase control signal oscillation. An intermediate value was sought 
for each channel independently. It was further verified through 
simulation that variations in $K_D$ had negligible influence on the 
system response; therefore, $K_D$ was fixed at a small value for all 
channels to provide light damping.

\subsection{UCC model-based}\label{model}

Currently, most of the UCC systems use vehicle models to obtain and adjust the parameters of the individual controllers. 

A widely adopted approach is the bicycle model, illustrated in Fig. \ref{fig:Modelo-bicicleta}. In this representation, $F_f$ and $F_r$ denote the lateral forces acting on the front and rear axles, respectively; $V_{ref}$ is the vehicle's velocity vector; and $\alpha_f$ and $\alpha_r$ are the slip angles of the front and rear wheels. The Center of Gravity (CG) is located at distances $l_f$ and $l_r$ from the front and rear axles. The side-slip angle, $\beta$, represents the relationship between the vehicle's longitudinal and lateral velocities, while $r = \dot{\psi}$ denotes the yaw rate. Additionally, $\delta_f$ epresents the front wheel steering angle, and $V_f$ and $V_r$ are the velocity vectors at the front and rear tires.\

This model is frequently integrated into Model Predictive Control (MPC) frameworks, as seen in \cite{Ahmadi2021}, \cite{Gidlewski2017}, and \cite{Yakub2016}. Other studies, such as \cite{Owen2018}, utilize it to analyze the impact of the center-of-mass location on vehicle performance, while \cite{Bilen2010} and \cite{Fahimi2013} employ it for computational simulations and controller design \citep{Xia2021}.

To achieve higher fidelity, some authors develop more complex models to capture intricate vehicle dynamics and subsystem interactions. For instance, \cite{Lai2021} introduced an 18-degree-of-freedom (DOF) model to represent comprehensive chassis dynamics. This model facilitates the design of a UCC system that evaluates the trade-off between ride comfort and handling stability across various maneuvers, accounting for the complex coupling between subsystems. The 18-DOF model treats the vehicle as five rigid bodies: a sprung mass (chassis) with 6-DOF (roll, pitch, yaw, and translational motions) and four unsprung masses (wheels). Each wheel is modeled with 3-DOF, corresponding to vertical displacement, wheel rotation, and steering rotation.

Similarly, \cite{Chen2021} proposed a 6-DOF rigid-body model that integrates planar motion (longitudinal, lateral, and yaw) with spatial motion (vertical, roll, and pitch). Combined with unsprung mass dynamics, this approach treats the vehicle as a unified system under longitudinal and lateral accelerations. To characterize nonlinear tire behavior, the authors utilize Pacejka’s Magic Formula, an empirical approach that unifies longitudinal force, lateral force, and self-aligning moment through parameter sets fitted to experimental data.

The use of model-based controls in vehicle dynamics offers the fundamental advantage of enabling precise and coordinated management of complex multivariable systems—such as the interaction between independent wheel drive and active suspension—facilitating the incorporation of physical constraints and the prediction of critical states to maximize safety and comfort \cite{Zihan2022}. However, this approach has critical drawbacks, primarily a high demand for computational resources to process high-fidelity models in real time \cite{Guo2020A}, as well as significant sensitivity to modeling errors (model mismatch) \cite{Lew2024Risk-Averse}. Any discrepancy between mathematical parameters and real-world conditions—such as variations in mass or unexpected changes in tire friction—can significantly degrade system performance and stability if robust adaptation mechanisms are not in place.

\end{figure}

\subsection{Uncertainty Compensation}
Given the limitations discussed above, neural network-based controllers have been widely used to compensate for model uncertainties and unmodeled nonlinearities in vehicle dynamics. This is the case in \cite{electronics10040510}, where the lateral dynamics are described using a dynamic bicycle model, capturing key variables such as longitudinal velocity, slip angle, yaw rate, and tire lateral forces, while also accounting for trajectory tracking errors and unmodeled external disturbances (e.g., crosswinds). To compensate for parametric uncertainties, nonlinearities, and unmodeled dynamics, a radial basis function neural network (RBFNN) is integrated into a higher-order sliding mode control (HOSMC) scheme. The RBFNN estimates the unknown dynamics online based on the sliding surface, with its parameters updated through Lyapunov-based adaptive laws. This combined approach enhances robustness against disturbances, reduces chattering, and preserves lateral control performance under adverse conditions.

In addition to neural network-based approaches, fuzzy logic has also been employed to handle parametric uncertainties within sliding mode control frameworks. This is the case in \cite{Guo2021}, where a fuzzy inference system is used to adaptively tune the boundary layer thickness based on the magnitude of the sliding surface, as part of a neuro-fuzzy adaptive sliding mode control strategy.

In this approach, the absolute value of the sliding surface is used as the input to the fuzzy system, while the boundary layer thickness is the output. Using predefined linguistic rules and membership functions, the controller adjusts this parameter online: large deviations from the sliding surface lead to a thinner boundary layer to ensure fast convergence, whereas small deviations result in a thicker layer to attenuate chattering.

This adaptive fuzzy mechanism enables an effective trade-off between robustness and oscillation reduction, improving control performance in the presence of parametric uncertainties and external disturbances.









One of the most widely used schemes for validating controller design in automotive systems is the CarSim\textsuperscript{\textregistered} software.  CarSim\textsuperscript{\textregistered} mathematical models are highly optimized for computational efficiency and are represented at the system level, meaning that vehicle data is intended to be measured or calculated and does not rely on detailed knowledge of component materials or suspension linkage complexities. When using a CarSim\textsuperscript{\textregistered} model, major issues may include the need for detailed vehicle data, accurate environmental conditions, and a good understanding of vehicle models at the system level. Users should also be aware of the importance of accurate kinematics and suspension compliance, tire strength and moment properties, and environmental conditions, as well as the potential impact of limited reference data on the accuracy of simulation results \citep{carsimintro2021}.\

Due to the above, the study of MFC for the regulation of vehicle dynamics in real-time is of interest. It should be noted that the term "Model-free control" can be seen in the literature as referring to a wide range of controllers that can go from "classical" proportional-integral-derivative (PID) to robust and adaptive control techniques derived, for example, from neural networks, fuzzy systems, and soft computing. The type of MFC used in this paper (intelligent PID based on ultra-local model) was presented by \cite{Fliess2008}, which makes it a novel technique and alternative to classical control in the automotive domain. Several authors have used this control technique as part of an automotive UCC system. In general, the MFC can be used in this field to regulate the longitudinal dynamics (direction and/or speed) of the vehicle \citep{Dong2014}, as a controller of the lateral dynamics (yaw rate and/or side slip angle) as in the case of \cite{Radac2018}, for the control of the joint lateral and longitudinal dynamics like in \cite{Menhour2018} and \cite{Rizk2023}, or for the control of the vertical dynamics, that is roll, roll rate and/or pitch \citep{Mustafa2019}.\

However, there are no references in the literature to works that use this control method to jointly regulate the lateral and vertical dynamics at the same time and in a single UCC system. In the present work, an automotive UCC system is designed using only MFC controllers to minimize the side-slip angle, reducing the yaw rate error and maintaining the vertical stability corresponding to the car roll angle and roll rate. The proposed system is tuned and validated through a Sine With Dwell maneuver in CarSim\textsuperscript{\textregistered} software. Those are original contributions, to the best of the authors' knowledge.

\section{The ultra-local model-based MFC controller} \label{sec:ulmodel}

The Model-Free Controller (MFC) based ultra-local model assumes that the system response will behave as shown in Eq. (\ref{Eq:Def-MFC}) for a small time window:

\begin{equation}
    y^{v} = F + \alpha u,
    \label{Eq:Def-MFC}
\end{equation}
where $y^{v}$ represents the $v${th} derivative of the output (usually $v = 1$ or $v = 2$) \citep{Fliess2009}, and $F$ is the input/output ratio of a simplified and little-known model of the plant. This parameter is a piecewise constant function that is updated in each time window and also includes measured and unmeasured perturbations of the plant.  On the other hand,  $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$ is a number that represents a non-physical constant parameter. This parameter is defined by the designer in such a way as to have the same order of magnitude as the unknown real parameter, and then an adjustment is made based on the system's time response. In this paper, there are three plant variables: yaw rate ($\dot{\psi}$), side-slip angle ($\beta$), and roll angle ($\phi$), where the last two ones are controlled using a second-order ultra-local model, while the first one is represented by a first-order ultra-local model.

Assuming that the model represents the plant dynamics in a short time window, then the stabilizing intelligent PID control (iPID) can be computed by: 
\begin{equation}
    u = \frac{-F}{\alpha}+\frac{\ddot{y^*}}{\alpha}+K_Pe+K_I\int e\,dt +K_D\frac{de}{dt},
    \label{Def-u-v2}
\end{equation}
where $\ddot{y^*}$ is the reference second time-derivative that the output is intended to follow, $e = y - y^*$ is the system tracking error and $K_P$, $K_I$ and $K_D$ are the gains of a conventional PID controller. These gains are adjusted to cancel out the measured output and impose the desired dynamics. It should be noted that this control can be in the form of a PI or a PD as appropriate. Finally, the control method involves estimating the value of $F$ in constant time windows. It can be demonstrated that even if the initial guess for $\alpha$ is incorrect, the estimator will correctly estimate the compensation of $F$ \citep{NA2022}. Therefore, $\alpha$ will only slightly affect the performance but will ensure the system stability.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{$F$ estimator to the ultra-local model of $\beta$ and $\phi$}
As mentioned earlier, the ultra-local models of $\beta$ and $\phi$ are considered of second order ($v=2$). In that case, the equation would be $\ddot{y} = F_2+\alpha u$. Applying the Laplace transform in this equation results in $s^2Y(s)-sy(0)-\dot{y}(0) = F_2/s+\alpha U(s)$. Then, by taking the derivative of the equation concerning $s$ twice, one obtains:
\begin{multline}
    2Y(s)+4s\frac{d}{ds}Y(s)+s^2\frac{d^2}{ds^2}Y(s) = \\ = 2s^{-3}F_2 + \alpha\frac{d^2}{ds^2}U(s).
    \label{Diff-Def-MFC-v2}
\end{multline}

Finally, multiplying Eq. (\ref{Diff-Def-MFC-v2}) by $\frac{1}{s^3}$ results in\footnote{This process ensures that integral terms appear in every factor, thereby aiding in noise reduction.}:
\begin{multline}
    2\frac{1}{s^3}Y(s)+4\frac{1}{s^2}\frac{d}{ds}Y(s)+\frac{1}{s}\frac{d^2}{ds^2}Y(s) = \\ = 2F_2\frac{1}{s^6}+\alpha\frac{1}{s^3}\frac{d^2}{ds^2}U(s).
    \label{Div-1s3-Def-MFC-v2}
\end{multline}

Returning to the time domain using the Laplace anti-transform, it is obtained:
\begin{multline}
    F_2 = \frac{60}{t^5}\int_{0}^t(t^2-6t\tau+6\tau^2)y(\tau)\,d\tau + \\ -\frac{30\alpha}{t^5}\int_{0}^t(t-\tau)^2\tau^2u(\tau)\,d\tau.
    \label{Antilaplace-Def-MFC-v2}
\end{multline}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{$F$ estimator to the ultra-local model of $\dot{\psi}$}

The ultra-local model of $\dot{\psi}$ is considered of first order ($v=1$). In that case, the equation would look like $\dot{y} = F_1+\alpha u$. The demonstration is very similar to the estimator of second order. Applying the Laplace transform in this equation results in $sY(s)-y(0) = F_1/s+\alpha U(s)$.
Then, by taking the derivative of the equation by $s$, one has:
\begin{equation}
    Y(s)+s\frac{d}{ds}Y(s)= -s^{-2}F_1+\alpha\frac{d}{ds}U(s).
    \label{Diff-Def-MFC-v1}
\end{equation}
Finally, multiplying the Eq. (\ref{Diff-Def-MFC-v1}) by $\frac{1}{s^2}$ results in:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{Y(s)}{s^2}+\frac{1}{s}\frac{d}{ds}Y(s)= -F_1+\frac{\alpha}{s^2}\frac{d}{ds}U(s).
    \label{Div-1s3-Def-MFC-v1}
\end{equation}

Upon returning to the time domain via the Laplace inverse transform, one obtains:
\begin{multline}
    F_1 = -\frac{6}{t^3}\int_{0}^t(t-2t\tau)y(\tau)\,d\tau + \\ - \frac{6\alpha}{t^3}\int_{0}^t(t-\tau)\tau u(\tau)\,d\tau.
    \label{Antilaplace-Def-MFC-v1}
\end{multline}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Discretization of algebraic estimators}

The computational application of the estimator is possible through the window integral approximation. For this, the size of the integration window ($T$) must be decided, where, in general, the larger the window, the more precise the estimated value will be. On the other hand, the control will be slower since the estimator will take longer to converge to the value. Considering the response speed of the car dynamics, the windows for all estimators were defined as $T=1.0$ s.

Considering the trapezoidal approximation of the integral, the estimators (of first and second order) can be written as:
\begin{multline}
    \hat{F}_2 = \frac{60}{T^5}\sum_{k=0}^{T/T_s} a_k(T^2-6TkT_s+6(kT_s)^2)y[k] + \\
    - \frac{30\hat{\alpha}}{T^5}\sum_{k=0}^{T/T_s} a_k(T-kT_s)^2(kT_s)^2u[k],
\end{multline}
and
\begin{multline}
    \hat{F}_1 = -\frac{6}{T^3}\sum_{k=0}^{T/T_s} a_k(T-2TkT_s)y[k] + \\
    - \frac{6\hat{\alpha}}{T^3}\sum_{k=0}^{T/T_s}a_k (T-kT_s)kT_s u[k],
\end{multline}
where:
\begin{equation}
    a_k = \left\{\begin{matrix}
    T_2/2, & k=0 \text{ and } k=T/T_s \\ 
    T_s, & k=1, \dots, T/T_s - 1
    \end{matrix}\right. .
\end{equation}

\subsection{Supervision layer}

The supervision layer aims to compute the references, as well as conditions under which the controller will act. The reference for $\beta$ is zero and it is calculated based on the estimated car lateral speed, as shown in Eq. (\ref{Eq:beta_def}). It is also defined that the controller for $\beta$ only performs whether it is outside the limits in Eq. (\ref{Eq:beta_lim}). In the case of the yaw rate, the controller's reference is calculated based in the longitudinal speed, the steering angle of the front wheels, and the coefficient of ground friction. The controller's operating range is determined by the limits described in Eq. (\ref{limYR}).  In the case of the roll angle, the reference will always be zero, as one of the controller's objectives is to reduce this parameter. The equations are: 
\begin{equation}
    \label{Eq:beta_def}
    \beta = {\rm tan}^{-1}(\frac{V_y}{V_x}),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:beta_lim}
\beta \le \beta_{lim} = \pm 2^{\circ},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{limYR}
    \dot{\psi}_D = \min \left( \left|\frac{\mu g}{V_x}\right|,\left|G_{\dot{\psi}}\delta_f\right|\right) \cdot {\rm sign}(\delta_f),
\end{equation}
where, $G_{\dot{\psi}} = V_x/(L+K_{us}V_x^2)$, $V_x$ is the car longitudinal speed, $L$ is the distance between the car's axles in meters and $K_{us}$ is the under-steer coefficient.

\subsection{High level controller}

The high-level controller design uses the references calculated by the supervision layer and the estimated states and calculates the torques needed to control the dynamics. In the case of lateral dynamics (which involves $\dot{\psi}$ and $\beta$), the control signal will be a torque around the car's z-axis ($M_z$). This signal is setting a saturation level of $\pm 8000$Nm. To control the roll angle, torque is calculated around the x-axis ($M_x$). This signal is limited by saturation levels of $\pm 1500$Nm. \subsection{Coordination layer}
As can be seen in Fig. \ref{Fig:UCC-implem}, in the coordination phase, a Pure Subsumption strategy is used to weigh up which of the control signals corresponding to $\beta$ and $\dot{\psi}$ will have more weight in the final torque calculation. This layer is not involved in controlling the roll angle.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Control allocation}

At this step, the control signals calculated in the previous steps are transformed into "understandable" variables by the subsystems that act directly on the car's chassis. In the case of the torque on the z-axis, it will be transformed into individual brake pressure signals for each wheel by the ABS. In the case of $M_x$, it will be transformed into suspension forces relating to the active stabilizer bar (AARB) system. This step is carried out directly by the CarSim\textsuperscript{\textregistered} software.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Individual actuators}

The individual brake pressure signals and the motor torque signal of the AARB system from the previous step are taken in this step as references for the controllers of the car's ABS and the active stabilizer bar system, respectively. The control of these systems finally makes it possible to establish the desired behavior of the controlled dynamics. Like the previous one, this stage is implemented by CarSim\textsuperscript{\textregistered} software.

\section{Control tuning} \label{sec:contr}


In the following subsections, a demonstration of how to adjust the parameters $\alpha$, $K_P$, and $K_D$ of the iPD proposed controllers is performed (in this paper, we consider the integrative factor equals to zero). It should be noted that this tuning is empirically done and that the dynamics to be controlled do not require high precision in tracking the corresponding references for the system to exhibit proper behavior.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{$\alpha$ adjustment}

This parameter is set individually for each control law, and has direct influence on the system outputs ($M_z$ and $M_x$ torques). Since the $M_z$ signal is composed of the sum of individual signals corresponding to the side-slip angle control and the yaw rate control, it is necessary to adjust simultaneously $\alpha_{\beta}$ and $\alpha_{\dot{\psi}}$. A bad choice of these parameters can result in an oscillatory control signal with a high saturation time and/or an unsatisfactory maneuver performance. In this work, the values of $\alpha_s$ are empirically obtained looking for a relationship between them and the time response of the controlled parameters. Higher $\alpha$ value can induce some delay in the system response while a lower $\alpha$ generates a more oscillatory control signal. The objective then is to find a equilibrium $\alpha$. The selected $\alpha$ values are  $\alpha_\beta = 0.01$,  $\alpha_{\dot{\psi}} = 10^{-3}$ and $\alpha_\phi = 10^{-2}$.


% ------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{iPD adjustment}

It was verified by simulations that the variation of the $K_d$ parameter does not have influence on the system response. The $K_p$ was adjusted firstly for the yaw rate and side-slip angle controllers and then for the roll control. The adjustment must keep a balance between reducing the steady state error and producing an oscillatory control signal. It should be noted that in the event of a sudden maneuver (as in the case of SWD) the control must prioritize the safety over the comfort. This is evidenced by the increase in the roll rate signal. Finally, the iPD adjustment is as follows: $Kp_{\beta} = 20$; $Kd_{\beta} = 3$; $Kp_{\dot{\psi}} = 50$; $Kd_{\dot{\psi}} = 3$; $ Kp_{\phi} = 200$; $Kd_{\phi} = 3$.

\section{Experiment design} \label{sec:exper}

To validate the designed UCC, it is simulated in Matlab/Simulink\textsuperscript{\textregistered} acting on the Carsim\textsuperscript{\textregistered} SUV model adapted to the parameters referred in \cite{Arronte2023} in a Sine With Dwell (SWD) maneuver. The results presented in this section focus on the comparison between the proposed iPD-based MFC controller, the gain-scheduled LQR benchmark, and the open-loop vehicle response under the same test conditions. To show the system's robustness, tests are performed three different $\mu$ values \citep{Ahrenhold2023}: $\mu = 0.7$ (Dry Asphalt Road), which simulates good grip and stability for vehicles, $\mu = 0.5$ (Wet Road), which corresponds to reduced traction due to a lubricating film of water, increasing the risk of skidding, and $\mu = 0.3$ (Icy Road), which simulates extremely low traction, and requires careful driving and often needs specialized tires or chains for safety.

The SWD maneuver involves accelerating the vehicle to a speed of just over 80 km/h without steering or braking, leaving it in the highest gear up to 80 km/h, and then using a robot to apply a steering control in the form of a sine wave with a dwell (momentary pause in the maximum steering angle). The purpose of the test is to evaluate the vehicle's response to a sudden evasive maneuver, such as swerving to avoid an obstacle, and to ensure compliance with regulations such as USA FMVSS 126 and UN/ECE Regulation No. 13-H \cite{MSC2012}. The system simulation is performed by using CarSim\textsuperscript{\textregistered} and Matlab\textsuperscript{\textregistered} together. Figure \ref{fig:sine-maneuver} shows the waveform of the steering signal in this maneuver.

\subsection{Statistical results across different friction conditions}

Table~\ref{tab3} presents a statistical analysis of the vehicle's dynamic response under a Sine With Dwell (SWD) maneuver, considering the same three different road friction coefficients: $\mu = 0.7$, $\mu = 0.5$, and $\mu = 0.3$. The results are reported for the two control strategies (gain-scheduled LQR and iPD MFC) as well as the open-loop case. The cases in which stability is not guaranteed are presented in \textbf{bold numbers}. 

\textbf{Side-slip angle:} Both controllers demonstrate a clear ability to reduce the side-slip angle. The MFC consistently achieves the lowest RMS and RMSE values, particularly at $\mu = 0.5$ and $\mu = 0.3$, where the gain-scheduled LQR benchmark shows slightly higher slip levels. At $\mu = 0.7$, MFC manages a lower RMS (0.627) compared to LQR (0.735), and at $\mu = 0.5$, this difference becomes more significant (MFC: 0.531 vs. LQR: 0.71). In the open-loop case, the side-slip angle increases substantially, peaking at 11.945° for $\mu = 0.5$, highlighting the importance of active control for maintaining lateral stability.

\textbf{Yaw rate:} In all conditions, the MFC achieves the lowest yaw rate RMS and RMSE values, followed by the gain-scheduled LQR benchmark. At $\mu = 0.7$, MFC maintains an RMS of 10.770 deg/s and a maximum yaw rate of 34.203 deg/s, while the uncontrolled vehicle reaches a peak of 49.98 deg/s. This pattern continues for $\mu = 0.5$, where MFC maintains better tracking and dampening of yaw motion. 

\textbf{Roll angle:} Roll behavior also improves with both controllers. At $\mu = 0.7$, the uncontrolled vehicle experiences an extreme peak roll angle of 79.993, while MFC and the gain-scheduled LQR limit this to 5.917° and 4.161°, respectively. At $\mu = 0.5$, the LQR benchmark shows superior performance in minimizing the roll angle (RMS: 0.222), while MFC reaches a higher RMS (1.260) but still provides acceptable control.

\textbf{Roll rate:} Similar to the roll angle, the roll rate is substantially reduced by the control strategies. At $\mu = 0.7$, the uncontrolled vehicle reaches a peak roll rate of 115.104 deg/s, whereas the LQR and MFC controllers keep it below 42 deg/s. The same trend is observed for $\mu = 0.5$, with the MFC showing higher values than LQR at $\mu = 0.5$.

For $\mu=0.3$, the gain-scheduled LQR benchmark and the open-loop case do not reach stability. Overall, the analysis demonstrates that both control strategies (gain-scheduled LQR and iPD MFC) significantly enhance vehicle stability and rollover mitigation during the SWD maneuver for $\mu=0.7$ and $\mu=0.5$, with the MFC generally offering superior performance in minimizing side-slip and yaw rate errors. The roll angle and roll rate are better under the LQR benchmark. On the other hand, the iPD MFC has better robustness of stability, as it does not fail in the case of $\mu=0.3$. The uncontrolled vehicle (open-loop) is not robustly stable.

This study proposes a novel Unified Chassis Control (UCC) system based on Model-Free Controllers (MFCs) to simultaneously regulate vehicle lateral (side-slip angle and yaw rate) and vertical (roll angle) dynamics during abrupt maneuvers. By leveraging ultra-local models and algebraic estimators, the MFC-based UCC eliminates the need for detailed vehicle modeling, effectively addressing challenges posed by parametric uncertainties and the nonlinear nature of traditional model-based approaches. The controller was evaluated through a Sine With Dwell (SWD) maneuver in a co-simulation environment using MATLAB/Simulink\textsuperscript{\textregistered} and CarSim\textsuperscript{\textregistered}, under varying road friction conditions ($\mu = 0.7$, $\mu = 0.5$, and $\mu = 0.3$), and compared against a gain-scheduled LQR benchmark. Results showed that the MFC-based UCC significantly reduced side-slip angle and yaw rate errors while maintaining good roll angle stability, ensuring comprehensive lateral and vertical control. In low-friction scenarios ($\mu = 0.3$), only the MFC showed stability. 

Future research should focus on automating the tuning using optimization to enhance scalability and precision, validating the approach through real-world implementations, and comparing it with alternative model-free techniques such as neural networks or fuzzy logic controllers. Additionally, extending the UCC framework to account for longitudinal dynamics or adapting it for diverse vehicle types, including electric and heavy-duty platforms, could further expand its applicability.
